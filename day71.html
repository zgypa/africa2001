Day 71 - Gedaref, Sudan

Friday August 3rd, 2001

I found the real Karkade. They serve them at any corner of the streets. And they have other stuff too..

And I went to change some money from the customs, and the Ethiopian kid was with me, and he saw where I put my money, which was in the jacket, and I forgot that they saw that, so I left my jacket inside a truck, but that wasn't a safe enough place. He opened the pocket, took out the check book, took my pen, and closed up again. He left my Visor. The Ethiopian bastard stole $200 inTC & ca $60 in cash with 2000 dinar. OH, well, there are greater losses in life, aren't they?

Finally we leave. I feel pretty bad. I know this trip can be hell. But it isn't raining, so I know that if it stays this way, I'll be good. All I need is a truck that does not brake and a dangerous driver, as they like to call them here. Dangerous used in a positive way: see, if somebody kicks ass at something, he's dangerous at that. And that's pretty true.

Well, OK, I'm in the bus writing know, writing with the stylus, and, well, the transmission shaft is scraping the ground. I guess it's OK, right? I mean what would you do if your transimission shaft of your car were to scrape the ground? Keep driving, right? Right. So that's what we do. After all we better keep driving until we just completely brake down, or we'll never get to were we can fix it.

Oh, look! The hub is leaking oil, and the transmission lever is broken, the starter's dead, the radiator is leaking and the brakes have problems, such that we must go slow.

What a beautiful scenary, though: A flock of white birds (you find them where you see cattle or water) crossing the street, a road on land, east Sudanese land.

Gassim (the tranportation guy, who gave me a ride on "his" trucks) invited me to his house, where I slept, and hoped it was a flat. I wonder how I could possibly think that. Maybe it just was in my conception that since he was sort of making some money, he would have a flat or something. But there is noone in flats. I mean for some reason, I haven't met anyone that lives in flats yet. I wonder... But instead it was a very clean mud-hut with toilet and shower outside without roaches or insects. Slept outide on one of those wooden bed frames that I have seen in Lalibela in the churhes for the fist time. It was good! Very good! Too bad there were mosquitos, but the sky was clear, I could see the starts, and I was in the hood, basically. Interesting staying there.
