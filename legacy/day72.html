Day 72 - Khartoum, Sudan

Saturday August 4th, 2001

Shueia = little (tnisr-tnish in amharic)
Tanam = good

19:10 The Sun is about to set on the Blue Nile. Khartoum is the city in Sudan where the Blue Nile meets the White Nile to form "The Nile", as we and the Egyptians know it. The sky is clear (what a surprise for the rainy season, but in Khartoum also the rainy season doesn't yield too much water), and the Sun is know half light orange, and half red-orange. It's got some line of clouds around it so that it looks like Saturn. But where exactly am I, really? Great Sunset. In the background Wimbledon 2001, or some sort of athletics olympics. But I know that the paradise will soon be over. As the susets, those Malaria-infested mosquitos will come out, but here by the Nile there are billions of them, so they say, because I have not slept here yet. But if one forgets about Malaria, (and one needs to do so in a place like this, although it is difficult at first. It comes natural to think of this place as Africa, and not as, oh say nature. But I have to compare all these things with what I am used to seeing.). Now the sun is like a moon, or the Full Moon, red and wrapped by the fog and humidity created by the Nile. The Moon has not risen yet though, which makes me once again wonder about that phenomenon that I have heard so many times: When you can't distinguish the Moon from the Sun.

Today I arrived in Khartoum by bus, a bus which was not the best bus, the famous El Saf-saf, but was a bus that cost me 1600 dinar, which is about $6.40, but it was an italian bus, and it looked and felt like a Greyhound. We all had numbered seats (unheard of around here but also around the States), and only 4 seats per row, unlike the other, say Ethiopian, buses, where there were 5 passangers per row.

I decided ot stay at the Blue Nile sailing club, where I could camp for $3/nite. But when I got off the bus, the taxi drivers where giving me the prices in Sudanese pounds, but I was counfused for a minute, and thought it was in dinar (funny because I knew about this problem), so they were asking me for 10,000 Sudanese pounds, which is now equal to 1,000 dinar, which is an OK price. But I laughed at them, when they told me 10,000, also because a) the bus ride from Gedaref to Khartoum (which takes like the 4-5 hours) costed 6-7 times less, and b) I didn't even have that much (10,000dinar) on me!

I got to the city with a minibus having carried all my stuff to the minibus in the sun, which was hot. I was then able to follow the lonely planet map of the city and find my way around to the Ministry of Tourism and Agricolture. Here I got travel permit, and zeroxs thereof,. Tomorrow I have to go register at the Immigration or whatever it's called here. I also have to do internet, but most importantly I have to call AmEx to cancel the stolen travellers checks, which were stolen from Muluye Bekele Kassa, PO Box 120, Bahar Dar, Ethiopia. I guess I will send him a post card and ask him what he did with all that material he stole from me.

Today I drank a lot of Soda. It's like it's hot, and the only thing you crave for is soda, which of course does not satisfy your thirst, because it's too sweet. But I went to a bar or rather little food store across the street from the sailing club and they had me try the so called (btw, the name of the apple soda was Stim, by Pepsi, and here in Sudan it seems that Pepsi took over) Ardeb, which is a cold tea made out of these sort of beans that are bitter, but also sweet and sticky and you can suck them, and they give this Ardeb flavour for a long time. The flavour seems to be on the outside of the shell and to be a very thick water-soluble layer. The plant grows only in South Sudan, where there is war. And it's not because of the war that it grows there, just in case you were wondering.

But by ending up in Sudan I fainlly reached a civilized place: people here have respect for you and for themselves. There are no beggars, or rather, no more than there are in Italy, and if somebody says hi to you on the street they won't start following you. Now I have the proof that it must not be that way. And Khartoum is absolutely fantastic: it has a lot of Africa with little tarmac streets, and mud huts in the outisde (just like Gedaref), but then somehow the people that live in it are not African, or at[...]
